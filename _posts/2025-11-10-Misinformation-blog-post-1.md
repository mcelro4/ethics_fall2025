**Article:**  
[Censorship of Misinformation and Freedom of Speech on Social Media](https://mit-serc.pubpub.org/pub/lkf63cu5/release/1)

Brief Summary

This case study presents the acts on censorship in social media and the misinformation that affects platforms coexisting with democratic rights. Kevin Mills explores the ways to mitigate misinformation online.

Discussion Questions:

This case study discusses several examples in which misinformation appears to be worse now than it was in the past (e.g., vaccine skepticism and election conspiracies). Can you think of any other examples? How confident are you that this misinformation really is worse now?

What strategies can you employ to avoid falling for misinformation online?

I can employ the strategy of assessing information based on who's saying it. Is it experts and/or is it someone trustworthy enough or communicable to express facts without opinion or bias. I can also research topics that have different viewpoints or seem shady. Thus, allowing me to fact check what i've seen.

If censorship were likely to be an effective tool in combatting misinformation on social media, would it be justified? Why or why not? And if so, in what cases and to what extent?

The article mentions scenarios where censorship is uncontroversially a good idea, like hate speech and "child pornography". There are subjects that as a larger group are agreed upon based on moral rights and wrongs. The area where it may not be justified is when people's opinions are taken away from them, socially. Political views, truth telling, or the spread of awareness sometimes is taken down by higher ups or people running apps based on what their views are which taps into rights of speech. This is where it may not be justified because people have the right to their opinion and when that is taken away it goes against the first amendment. Ultimately, I think it would be justified if people educated themselves to know whether information is false.
